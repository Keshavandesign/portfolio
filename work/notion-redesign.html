Unsolicited Redesign Product Thinking Onboarding

Notion gives you
everything. That's the problem.

Notion has 30M+ users and a week-one churn problem. The blank canvas that power users love is the same thing that makes new users feel lost. This is a focused redesign of the first-use experience: not the UI, the psychology.

The problem

Power is a liability
in week one.

Notion's flexibility is its biggest product differentiator and its biggest onboarding failure. I audited the first-use flow and found three moments where new users consistently abandon: the empty workspace, the new page creation, and the sidebar that immediately becomes a junk drawer.

What I found

Three insights that
shaped the redesign.

01
Structure reduces anxiety, not freedom Users don't want infinite flexibility on day one. They want a scaffold they can break later. Opinionated defaults don't cage people. They give them somewhere to start from.
02
The sidebar is a trust signal Within two weeks, a chaotic sidebar makes Notion feel broken. Users don't blame themselves. They blame the product. A sidebar that stays organised signals a product that has thought about them.
03
Type before name Asking "what do you want to call this?" before "what is this?" is backwards. Users think in types (doc, table, board) before they think in names. Flipping the order reduces abandonment.

"The blank canvas isn't a UX problem. It's a psychological one. People don't fear empty space. They fear making the wrong choice with it."

Observation from 6 user interviews, new Notion users, week one

The redesign

Three changes.
One clear principle.

Opinionated defaults, escapable constraints. Guide new users into a shape they understand. Then get out of the way as soon as they're ready to break it.

I chose structural scaffolding over onboarding wizards because walkthroughs have ~30% completion and disappear the moment the user closes them. A pre-populated workspace is passive: users can ignore it and still benefit from seeing what an organised Notion looks like on day one.

Change 01

Sidebar that
stays organised

Section headers group content automatically: Pinned (what you always need), Recent (what you're working on), Team (shared spaces), Archive (everything else).

Users can still name and arrange everything freely. The sections are the scaffold, not a cage.

Change 02

Pick a shape
before a name

When creating a new page, show what it could be before asking what to call it. Doc, Database, Board, Calendar, Checklist: six tiles, one tap.

The name field appears after. This removes the blank-name paralysis entirely. You already know what kind of thing you're making.

Change 03

A workspace that
feels inhabited

New workspaces start pre-populated: a pinned Home page, a Projects database stub, and a brief welcome panel showing 3 steps to get going. All deletable, but there when you need them.

The goal is to make the workspace feel like it already belongs to you on day one, not like something you have to earn.

Decisions made

What I
didn't build.

Every redesign is as much about what you reject as what you ship. These are the ideas I ruled out and why. Each one is a constraint that sharpens the solution.

Not built

AI-generated page templates on first open

Adds cognitive load before the user knows if they want AI in their workflow. The goal is confident starts, not clever automation. A scaffold the user can break is more respectful of how people learn than a wizard that decides for them.

Not built

Forced walkthrough / onboarding modal

Linear walkthroughs have around 30% completion and create a sense of homework. A pre-populated workspace is passive: users can ignore it entirely and still have a better starting point than a blank canvas.

Not built

Forcing the type picker for all new pages

Power users creating quick notes via keyboard shortcuts shouldn't be interrupted. The type picker only appears when creating a page from the empty main panel, not from ⌘+N or the sidebar. Mode matters.

What this
would move.
