Mini Project Spotify Personal Music Experience

Spotify knows your taste.
It just hides it from you.

You've liked 4,700 songs over six years. Spotify has perfect data on all of them. But the Liked Songs list shows them newest-first in a flat scroll with no context, no eras, no way to navigate your own collection. Discovery pulls you forward. Your history doesn't exist.

Type

Mini Project · 2 days

Problem

Liked Songs is unnavigable

Root cause

No temporal or contextual structure

The real problem

A flat list doesn't scale
to 4,700 songs.

Liked Songs is the most personal playlist on Spotify. It spans years of life events, phases, and moods. But it's navigated exactly like a newly-created playlist: newest first, no grouping, no signal about what's buried at the bottom.

Spotify invests heavily in algorithmic discovery to pull you toward new music. But there's no parallel investment in helping you navigate what you already own. Your collection grows every year and becomes less usable every year.

01
Recency always wins
The newest saves play first. Songs from 2019 (music you loved enough to save) effectively disappear. The shuffle function is random, not intentional.
02
Context evaporates
You save in context ("this is my gym song," "this fits that road trip"), but when you return, that context is gone. It's just a title and an artist. You've lost the thread.
03
Discovery vs. rediscovery
Spotify's entire recommendation engine pushes new content. There is no "rediscover what you already love" system. Algorithmic playlists rarely resurface your own Liked Songs.

"I have 3,000 liked songs. I basically just use Spotify Radio because I can't find anything in my actual library anymore."

Synthesised from 9 people with Liked Songs libraries over 1,000 tracks

The redesign

Your Collection.
Navigable by era.

Instead of a flat list, Your Collection groups music by the year you saved it, creating "eras" of your taste that are browsable like albums. A separate Forgotten Favorites panel surfaces songs you loved and lost. No AI, no magic. Just structure that was always possible with the data Spotify already has.

I chose temporal grouping over mood or genre because it maps to how people actually remember saving music: by life period, not abstract category. "My 2021 playlist" means something personal. "Sad indie rock" is a Spotify construct, not a memory.

The Problem

4,700 songs.
One flat scroll.

Open Liked Songs today. The list starts with what you saved last week. It ends with a song from 2018. There's no chapter structure, no way to say "show me what I liked in my final year of college." No grouping by era, mood, or energy.

Songs at the bottom have a badge that might as well say "forgotten." The interface has no mechanism to resurface them except random shuffle.

The Redesign

Your Collection.
Three ways in.

The new "Your Collection" view groups Liked Songs by year saved. Each era is a visual chapter: a compact block with an ambient collage of that year's album art. Tap an era to browse it as a focused playlist.

A persistent "Forgotten Favorites" section at the bottom surfaces songs liked before last year that you haven't played in 6+ months, with a single tap to queue them up again.

Decisions made

What I
left out.

The ideas I considered and rejected. Each rejection is a clarity decision. It tells you more about the design than what made it in.

Not built

Mood and energy tagging

Requires users to label their saves, a behavior Spotify can't guarantee at scale. Era grouping is zero-friction: the save timestamp is already there. Good design uses data that already exists before asking users to create new data.

Not built

AI-generated "taste chapters"

I considered letting an algorithm define your eras: "Your Indie Phase", "Your Workout Year." Rejected it. The point is to let your own memory do the navigating. The design should reference your experience, not interpret it for you.

Not built

Listen history editing / "I don't like this anymore"

A way to un-mark songs from old eras. Left out intentionally. The Forgotten Favorites surface is enough. Letting users edit their history introduces complexity and doesn't solve the core problem, which is navigation, not curation.
