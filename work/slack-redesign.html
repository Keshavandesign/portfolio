Mini Project Slack Information Architecture

Coming back to Slack
feels like punishment.

You step away for two hours. You return to 340 unread messages across 18 channels. The actual signal — one blocker, two decisions — is buried somewhere in there. This isn't a notification problem. It's an information architecture failure.

Type

Mini Project · 2 days

Problem

Catch-up experience after absence

Root cause

All messages are equal — none are

The real problem

Slack treats every message
as equally urgent. They're not.

A red badge means "unread." That's all it means. A meme in #random and a prod outage in #incidents both look identical in the sidebar. Slack's information model has no urgency, no relationship, no context — just a count.

The catch-up experience is "scroll up until you feel okay." There's no way to answer the only question that matters when you return: did anything actually need me?

01
No triage layer
Slack surfaces everything. "Everything" is noise. Without a triage layer, the user is the triage layer — every time, manually, at cognitive cost.
02
Badges reward clearing
The red badge creates anxiety until it's gone. Users scroll to clear the badge, not to find signal. The UI incentivises the wrong behaviour.
03
Absence has no cost model
Slack has no concept of "you were away." The same interface greets you after 2 hours as after 2 weeks. The catch-up experience doesn't scale with absence.

"The problem isn't that there are too many messages. It's that the interface makes every message look like it might be the one that matters."

Synthesised from conversations with 8 people who described Slack as "stressful"

The redesign

Catch-Up Mode —
signal, not scroll.

A new surface that activates when you've been away. Not a summary. Not AI magic. Just a structured triage view that groups what you missed into three buckets — so you can process it in order of what actually matters.

The Problem

340 unread.
No way in.

The current sidebar gives you a count. Each channel has a red badge. You have to open each one, scan, and decide if anything needed you. There's no shortcut. No priority. Just a list.

Users develop coping strategies — muting channels, ignoring badges — that defeat the purpose of the tool entirely.

The Redesign

Catch-Up Mode.
Three buckets.

When you return after an absence, Slack offers Catch-Up Mode: a single view of everything you missed, sorted into Needs you (mentions + DMs), Happening now (active threads), and When you're ready (the rest).

You process top-down. You reply inline. You mark buckets done. No channel hopping. The badge count goes to zero because you actually processed it — not because you scrolled past it.
