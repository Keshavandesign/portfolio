BuyingTeams

Banks and their business partners were drowning in data and still flying blind. Three months, five phases, one mandate: make complex performance metrics feel like a conversation not a spreadsheet.

Scroll
Overview
BuyingTeams was solving a real problem in B2B fintech: banks and their business partners had a relationship, but no shared view of how it was performing. Metrics lived in exports, emails, and memory. The platform needed to surface the right numbers to the right people and make acting on them feel natural, not laboured.
Core Challenge
The core blocker wasn't data there was plenty of it. It was access and context. A relationship manager needed a 2-day reporting cycle to answer a question that should take 20 seconds. The design problem: how do you collapse that gap without overwhelming users who aren't analysts?

Research
Phase 01

Research

Identifying friction points & validating hypotheses

Synthesize →
User Interviews

Spent the first two weeks just listening. Five relationship managers and business development leads walked me through their weekly ritual: export a CSV, paste into Excel, format it for a meeting, repeat. It was painful to watch. The platform didn't need to be clever it needed to replace something they genuinely hated.

Three friction points kept surfacing across every conversation: delayed reporting cycles (manual exports every time), information fragmentation (data split across 3+ systems), and lack of context (scores that told you nothing about why).

Surveys

A quantitative pass confirmed the pattern: 78% of respondents manually consolidated partner data from multiple sources every week. That number became the north star every design decision was held against it. Would this feature reduce that 78%?

Jobs to be Done
Core user motivations
  • Prepare for partner meetings with real-time performance context
  • Identify early warning signals in partner relationships
  • Benchmark performance against peer institutions
  • Make data-backed decisions on partnership continuation or escalation
Critical Friction Points
Barriers to adoption
  • Data silos: 78% manually consolidate from 3+ systems weekly
  • Comparison friction: No native ability to benchmark across time/geography
  • Learning curve: Complex tools abandoned for spreadsheets
  • Timeliness: 2-3 day reporting lag costs decision velocity
Success Metrics
What would win
  • Speed: Real-time access to replace 2-day reporting cycle
  • Accessibility: Non-technical users can navigate without training
  • Authority: Single source of truth eliminates spreadsheet sprawl
  • Action: Insights lead to measurable partnership decisions

Key Features

Derived from research synthesis and stakeholder feedback, these strategic features address the three core friction points identified in user interviews. Each was validated through card sorting (4 participants) to ensure information hierarchy aligned with user mental models.

Core Features
  • Unified Dashboard: Aggregated view of all partner metrics in one system
  • Geo & Product Filtering: Drill down by country, product line, business unit
  • Performance Comparison: Side-by-side analysis across time periods and peer groups
  • Feedback Loop: Structured mechanism for banks to respond to partner feedback
  • Data Export: One-click reporting for board presentations and strategic planning
Business Impact
  • Relationship Acceleration: Data-driven meetings reduce negotiation cycles
  • Risk Mitigation: Early warning signals on partner health prevent sudden disruptions
  • Revenue Opportunity: Cross-sell potential identified through performance trends
  • Competitive Intelligence: Peer benchmarking reveals market positioning gaps
  • Customer Insight: Consolidated feedback drives product development priorities

Ideate

Validating mental models & IA through user research

← Synthesize UI Design →
Focus Points Card Sorting

Put 4 power users in front of card stacks categories unlabeled, items unsorted. Watched where they naturally grouped things. 85% landed on the same primary structure without any coordination. That's when you know you're building on real mental models, not assumptions. The IA we shipped looked almost exactly like what those four users drew.

"Fintech interfaces often punish new users. If you need training to find your dashboard, the product has already failed. The 85% agreement told us we'd built navigation that felt like recognition not something that needed to be learned."

Site Map

Information architecture designed for progressive disclosure: primary dashboard aggregates all critical partner metrics, with drill-down screens allowing deep analysis by performance category. This structure reduced user navigation steps by 40% compared to flat menu approaches.

Task Flow

Task flow mapping exposed a split: executives need the 30,000-foot view portfolio trends, geographic performance, year-over-year. Operations teams need to drill into the weeds customer tier, industry segment, individual partner scores. Same product, opposite journeys. The dual-filter system was born from that tension, validated through usability testing with 6 participants.

Phase 04

UI Design

Iterative testing & refinement for clarity & trust

← Ideate Final Screens →
Wireframes

Low-fidelity prototyping prioritized testing layout hypotheses and interaction flows before visual design investment. Conducted 3 rounds of iterative testing (4 users per round), identifying and fixing 8 critical UX issues: filter affordance, metric grouping hierarchy, and drill-down navigation patterns. This approach reduced development rework by 25%.

Style Tile

Blue (#4358F4) because trust is the product. Fintech UI defaults to cold and clinical I wanted this to feel authoritative but approachable. Rounded corners, soft depth, generous white space. The kind of interface that makes someone confident before they've read a single number. WCAG AA across all text not box-ticking, but actual readability for users presenting on mediocre conference-room projectors.
